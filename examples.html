Examples

Some examples of analysis results and their interpretations.

Community evolution

It is well known that communication between contributors is a crucial factor for the success of a software development project. The figures on the right show how the communities of qemu have evolved over time (dots represent contributors, and lines the collaboration strength. The larger the dots, the more central the role of a contributor): During the early phases, the project was driven by few central developers. In recent times, the project has developed a professional development community, with roughly equally sized and shaped sub-communities without single central developers respectively points of failure.

Community structure for Qemu, early project phase.

Discussion culture

To determine the discussion culture on public mailing lists, we derive initiation-response diagrams that measure how many threads a given contributor initiated during a development cycle, and how many responses he/she received on average. Besides the raw numbers, the sizes of the dots indicate the centrality of a developer in the mailing list network, that is, how important he of she is for network structure. Consider the Apache httpd and OpenSSL projects For Apache project, numerous contributors with little influence produce a substantial amount of traffic, while the OpenSSL community has a larger selection of medium to highly important contributors. While a crypto library is typically only directly used by experts, a web server is of much more general interest, which attracts more non-specialist parties to contribute to discussions. Discussions in Apache are typically also more voluminous when it comes to the number of replies as compared to the openssl state, which is another indicator for a general interest versus expert community.


Connecting authors and topics

Not only the amount of communication, but also the topics discussed on the mailing list are of interest. We obtain these data by employing text mining methods that detect the most frequent keywords in discussions and which authors collaborate on these topics; the example shows data for the OpenSSL mailing list.

Time zone analysis

Git records author and committer time zone information, which allows for determining in which parts of the world development is done (or at least at which longitude). Both projects are truly international, albeit qemu tends to have a more pronounced focus on Europa than the Linux kernel, which also sees very active contributions from east and west America.

Engineering rigidity

Even simple technical metrics like the covariates associated with commits can deliver interesting information when examined properly: The figure on the right hand side shows release-resolved commit properties for the Linux kernel that additionally distinguish between development and stabilisation (release candidate) phases. Thanks to the project's disciplined engineering approach, code integrated during stabilisation phases is consistently smaller, has more comments associated with it, and touches less files than during active development. This makes the Linux kernel a reliable and trustworthy project.

Consistency

When a project is supposed to be used as a component of a larger project, it is important to know how reliable the release schedule and the behaviour within a release cycle is. Compare how changes (black line, releases are indicated by red lines) flow cumulatively into the Linux kernel (left) and jQuery (right).

Independent of any absolute quality notions that can be subjective, it is possible to determine how self-consistent a project is with regard to its development behaviour. We can measure this by determining how similar adjacent release cycle time series are (0 for perfectly identical, 1 for totally different). In addition to the Linux kernel and jQuery, the comparison graph also includes Qemu.
